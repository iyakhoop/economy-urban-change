Key Questions

  • In what ways did industrial capitalism affect the structure of cities?

Important Timeline

  • US: The Declaration of Independence 1776; Constitution 1787; First President 1789
  • FR: The French Revolution 1789
  • TH: Ayutthaya Kingdom: 1350-1767; Rattanakosin Kingdom: 1782-1932

Late Medieval Europe: 1300-1500

  • Low population growth: famine, war, plague
  • Population decline in Tuscany (Italy), Normandy (NW France), Provence (SE France)
  • Black Death: wipe out 40% of European population

Preindustrial City

  • City has a small population, surrounded by wall
  • City has three classes: Elite, Lower Class, Outcasts
  • Status declines with distance from the core
  • Elite class – religious and political leaders, high-ranking officers – lives in a central core
  • Merchants and craftmen live next to the core
  • Poor and minority groups are pushed to the outskirts of the city

The rise of urbanization in Western Europe

  • The Great Divergence (1500-1800): period of sustained growth and income per capita
  • Atlantic Traders: Britain, France, the Netherlands, Portugal, Spain
  • Colonialism and Slavery
  • The rise of merchant groups outside the royal circle

Industrial Revolution: Overview

  • Began in Britain during 1760 to 1840
  • Change from an agrarian to the industrial economy
  • New Source of energy: Coal and Steam engine
  • New Source of materials: Iron and Steel
  • Factory system: Mass production, Labor-saving technology, Division of labor
  • Reduced transaction costs: Canal, Railroad

Industrial Revolution: Socioeconomic Preconditions

  • Enclosure and labor movement
  • Agricultural improvements
  • Currency system: trade and exchange
  • Modern banking: loan and investment
  • New thinking in science, philosophy, and culture
  • Political institutions: Constitutional Monarchy; Laissez-fair economic policy; property rights

Industrial City: Early Stage

  • 1700-1780: new inventions in textiles, cotton, wool, and iron industry
  • These industries mostly located in the countryside because they need water to power the mill

Industrial City: Prime Stage

  • Coal: The rise of industrial towns
    • Yorkshire, Lancashire, Staffordshire, Durham
  • The first steam-driven railway in 1825
  • Old medieval towns become the major centers of the new factory industry
    • Leicester, Nottingham, Bristol
  • Rapid growth of port towns
    • Liverpool, Manchester, Hull, Glasgow, London

American Industrial Development

  • The abundance of land and scarcity of labor
  • New England's textile mills (1790-1850)
  • Whitney’s 1793 invention of the cotton gin
    • Cotton-producing industry in the South
  • Erie Canal (1817-1840): connecting Hudson River and Lake Erie
  • Rapid urbanization in New York City and Chicago

Industrial Society

  • Creation of new social groups
    • Industrial capitalists (Bourgeoisie)
    • Unskilled factory workers (Proletariat)
    • The middle class: Professional workers such as doctors, lawyer, teachers, journalists
  • Materialistic value: accumulation of wealth becomes the status of power
    • The new rich and conspicuous consumption

Spatial Structure of The Classic Industrial City

  • Seperation of classes
  • Pattern of Concentric Zones
    • Innermost: Overcrowded, Poverty
    • The middle zone: lower-middle class
    • The suburb: the wealthy class
